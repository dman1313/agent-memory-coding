## Blog

Stage two of my thinking is all about AI, agentic frameworks, and what a real teacher support system could look like when it’s actually tied to curriculum and day-to-day classroom life.

If you’ve been following AI lately, you’ve probably noticed a big push toward something that looks a lot like a wiki. We all remember wikis from back in the day, but the idea is coming back for a reason: a wiki-style setup is great for storing information, making connections, and helping both humans and AI “remember” what matters.

Over the last year, I’ve been working on an agentic framework where you can take policy and procedures, curriculum documents, lesson materials, and planning notes, and put them into an agent’s memory. The goal isn’t just to dump files into a tool. The goal is to give the agent enough context to work with you—helping you plan, clarify learning outcomes, and coach you through decisions as the year unfolds.

Here’s what that looks like in a real example. Say I’m a Grade 2 teacher using a math program. I upload our curriculum outcomes, our program of inquiry, and my own notes. I add meeting notes. I add reflections from lessons that went well—and lessons that didn’t. Yes, it can include sensitive information, which is why how this is built (privacy, access, storage) has to be taken seriously from day one.

Once the agent has digested that information, I can use it like a thinking partner. I might say, “I taught a fractions lesson and it didn’t go so well. Here are my notes. Can you give me advice on how to improve it?” Instead of giving generic tips, the agent can respond based on my context: what I’ve already tried, what the curriculum expects, what students struggled with, and what I’m teaching next.

This is where it starts to feel powerful for long-range planning. I might realize, “The kids didn’t really get fractions right now, and honestly, I’m not sure they’re developmentally ready. I want to come back to it later.” Then the agent can help me connect that decision to the rest of my year: “You’ve got a unit on sustainability, farms, markets, and food systems. What if you build fractions, data, and percentages into that unit?” Suddenly, fractions isn’t an isolated skill—it’s connected to real contexts like recipes, percentage loss, comparing quantities, or even looking at things like how much cream is in milk.

What I really like is that the agent doesn’t have to pretend it came up with the idea. If it’s my idea, that’s fine. The key is that it captures it. It stores the decision in memory so when I come back to that unit later, it can remind me: “Here’s the activity you planned. Here’s what you were thinking. Here’s the reason you chose this approach.” That’s the kind of support that actually reduces mental load.

It can also work as a practical reminder system, because classrooms are busy and teachers forget things—not because we don’t care, but because there are a thousand decisions every day. The agent could say, “Your Farm to Table unit is coming up. You planned a field trip. Do you want help writing the parent letter?” Or, “Here’s a checklist of what needs to happen this week: permission forms, admin approval, payment info, allergy notes.” Then you check it off and keep moving.

When I imagine this done well, it’s basically a personal assistant that understands teaching. And the best part is: this is doable now. It doesn’t take a coding genius to start building something useful, especially if you focus on the workflow teachers actually have—curriculum, planning, reflection, communication, and follow-through.

That’s the project I’m thinking about for next year. I’m excited to build it, test it in real conditions, and hopefully find partners who care about this problem too—because using AI to support teachers should be practical, safe, and grounded in what classrooms really need.

In the next blog, I’m going to push this further and talk about AI as a coach for individual learners—how it can support differentiation at scale using UDL, helpful reminders, and the kind of day-to-day scaffolding that students (and teachers) benefit from most.
